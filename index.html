Ukraine War

▸updated

Ukraine launches largest drone strike of the war on Moscow

Ukraine hit the Dubna satellite communications center and further damaged an oil refinery struck two days earlier. Seventeen people were injured and hundreds of flights were suspended at Moscow airports. Separately, the EU released a further $4.44bn tranche of its $101bn loan program for Ukrainian drone technology.

Source: Washington Post, CFR Global Conflict Tracker

▸updated

Overnight strike on Kyiv kills 20, damages 30+ sites

A Russian air attack overnight damaged more than 30 sites in Kyiv, killing at least 20 people and injuring 90 more, with further strikes in Poltava and Dnipropetrovsk oblasts. Ukrainian drones hit back at the Lukoil refinery in Kstovo and a railway bridge near Stanytsia Luhanska. European officials read the intensified bombing as a sign Russia is struggling militarily and economically, not a sign of strength.

Source: Washington Post, CFR Global Conflict Tracker

▸updated

Front nearly stalemated; casualty ratio now ~8:1 against Russia

Russia's ground advance has slowed to some of the lowest rates of any war in the past century — roughly 50m/day near Kostiantynivka, 70m/day near Pokrovsk, 90m/day near Sloviansk. Russian monthly casualties (30,000+) now likely exceed its recruitment (about 27,000/month), pushing the casualty ratio to near 8:1 against Russia in H1 2026. Ukrainian forces raised a flag over the Kinburn Spit for the first time since March 2022.

Source: CSIS, CFR Global Conflict Tracker

▸

ISW: Russian summer offensive underperforming

The Institute for the Study of War assesses that Russia's spring-summer 2026 offensive has failed to achieve operationally significant gains, with June's rate of advance a fraction of June 2025's — consistent with the stalled-front picture above.

Source: Institute for the Study of War

June jobs report shows a softening US labor market

Payrolls rose just 57,000, well below the 113,000 forecast. Unemployment actually fell to 4.2%, but mainly because 720,000 people left the labor force rather than because hiring picked up — a distinction that matters for reading the headline rate.

Source: Saxo Bank Market Quick Take
